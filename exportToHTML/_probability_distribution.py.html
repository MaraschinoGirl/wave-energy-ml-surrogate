6        @abstractmethod
7        def support(self):
8            r"""Support of the random variable 
9     
10           The support of a random variable is set of all possible outcomes; 
11           i.e., the subset of the domain of argument :math:`x` for which 
12           the probability density function :math:`f(x)` is nonzero. 
13    
14           This function returns lower and upper bounds of the support. 
15    
16           Returns 
17           ------- 
18           out : tuple of Array 
19               The lower and upper bounds of the support. 
20    
21           See Also 
22           -------- 
23           pdf 
24    
25           References 
26           ---------- 
27           .. [1] Support (mathematics), *Wikipedia*, 
28                  https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Support_(mathematics) 
29    
30           Notes 
31           ----- 
32           Suppose a continuous probability distribution has support ``(l, r)``. 
33           The following table summarizes the value returned by methods 
34           of ``ContinuousDistribution`` for arguments outside the support. 
35    
36           +----------------+---------------------+---------------------+ 
37           | Method         | Value for ``x < l`` | Value for ``x > r`` | 
38           +================+=====================+=====================+ 
39           | ``pdf(x)``     | 0                   | 0                   | 
40           +----------------+---------------------+---------------------+ 
41           | ``logpdf(x)``  | -inf                | -inf                | 
42           +----------------+---------------------+---------------------+ 
43           | ``cdf(x)``     | 0                   | 1                   | 
44           +----------------+---------------------+---------------------+ 
45           | ``logcdf(x)``  | -inf                | 0                   | 
46           +----------------+---------------------+---------------------+ 
47           | ``ccdf(x)``    | 1                   | 0                   | 
48           +----------------+---------------------+---------------------+ 
49           | ``logccdf(x)`` | 0                   | -inf                | 
50           +----------------+---------------------+---------------------+ 
51    
52           For the ``cdf`` and related methods, the inequality need not be 
53           strict; i.e. the tabulated value is returned when the method is 
54           evaluated *at* the corresponding boundary. 
55    
56           The following table summarizes the value returned by the inverse 
57           methods of ``ContinuousDistribution`` for arguments at the boundaries 
58           of the domain ``0`` to ``1``. 
59    
60           +-------------+-----------+-----------+ 
61           | Method      | ``x = 0`` | ``x = 1`` | 
62           +=============+===========+===========+ 
63           | ``icdf(x)`` | ``l``     | ``r``     | 
64           +-------------+-----------+-----------+ 
65           | ``icdf(x)`` | ``r``     | ``l``     | 
66           +-------------+-----------+-----------+ 
67    
68           For the inverse log-functions, the same values are returned for 
69           for ``x = log(0)`` and ``x = log(1)``. All inverse functions return 
70           ``nan`` when evaluated at an argument outside the domain ``0`` to ``1``. 
71    
72           Examples 
73           -------- 
74           Instantiate a distribution with the desired parameters: 
75    
76           >>> from scipy import stats 
77           >>> X = stats.Uniform(a=-0.5, b=0.5) 
78    
79           Retrieve the support of the distribution: 
80    
81           >>> X.support() 
82           (-0.5, 0.5) 
83    
84           For a distribution with infinite support, 
85    
86           >>> X = stats.Normal() 
87           >>> X.support() 
88           (-inf, inf) 
89    
90           Due to underflow, the numerical value returned by the PDF may be zero 
91           even for arguments within the support, even if the true value is 
92           nonzero. In such cases, the log-PDF may be useful. 
93    
94           >>> X.pdf([-100., 100.]) 
95           array([0., 0.]) 
96           >>> X.logpdf([-100., 100.]) 
97           array([-5000.91893853, -5000.91893853]) 
98    
99           Use cases for the log-CDF and related methods are analogous. 
100   
101          """
102          raise NotImplementedError()

107   
108          Parameters 
109          ---------- 
110          shape : tuple of ints, default: () 
111              The shape of the sample to draw. If the parameters of the distribution 
112              underlying the random variable are arrays of shape ``param_shape``, 
113              the output array will be of shape ``shape + param_shape``. 
114          method : {None, 'formula', 'inverse_transform'}

120              - ``'inverse_transform'``: generate a uniformly distributed sample and

170      @abstractmethod
171      def moment(self, order, kind, *, method):
172          r"""Raw, central, or standard moment of positive integer order. 
173   
174          In terms of probability density function :math:`f(x)` and support 
175          :math:`\chi`, the "raw" moment (about the origin) of order :math:`n` of 
176          a random variable :math:`X` is: 
177   
178          .. math:: 
179   
180              \mu'_n(X) = \int_{\chi} x^n f(x) dx 
181   
182          The "central" moment is the raw moment taken about the mean, 
183          :math:`\mu = \mu'_1`: 
184   
185          .. math:: 
186   
187              \mu_n(X) = \int_{\chi} (x - \mu) ^n f(x) dx 
188   
189          The "standardized" moment is the central moment normalized by the 
190          :math:`n^\text{th}` power of the standard deviation 
191          :math:`\sigma = \sqrt{\mu_2}` to produce a scale invariant quantity: 
192   
193          .. math:: 
194   
195              \tilde{\mu}_n(X) = \frac{\mu_n(X)} 
196                                      {\sigma^n} 
197   
198          Parameters 
199          ---------- 
200          order : int 
201              The integer order of the moment; i.e. :math:`n` in the formulae above. 
202          kind : {'raw', 'central', 'standardized'} 
203              Whether to return the raw (default), central, or standardized moment 
204              defined above. 
205          method : {None, 'formula', 'general', 'transform', 'normalize', 'quadrature', 'cache'} 
206              The strategy used to evaluate the moment. By default (``None``), 
207              the infrastructure chooses between the following options, 
208              listed in order of precedence. 
209   
210              - ``'cache'``: use the value of the moment most recently calculated 
211                via another method 
212              - ``'formula'``: use a formula for the moment itself 
213              - ``'general'``: use a general result that is true for all distributions 
214                with finite moments; for instance, the zeroth raw moment is 
215                identically 1 
216              - ``'transform'``: transform a raw moment to a central moment or 
217                vice versa (see Notes) 
218              - ``'normalize'``: normalize a central moment to get a standardized 
219                or vice versa 
220              - ``'quadrature'``: numerically integrate according to the definition 
221   
222              Not all `method` options are available for all orders, kinds, and 
223              distributions. If the selected `method` is not available, a 
224              ``NotImplementedError`` will be raised. 
225   
226          Returns 
227          ------- 
228          out : array 
229              The moment of the random variable of the specified order and kind. 
230   
231          See Also 
232          -------- 
233          pdf 
234          mean 
235          variance 
236          standard_deviation 
237          skewness 
238          kurtosis 
239   
240          Notes 
241          ----- 
242          Not all distributions have finite moments of all orders; moments of some 
243          orders may be undefined or infinite. If a formula for the moment is not 
244          specifically implemented for the chosen distribution, SciPy will attempt 
245          to compute the moment via a generic method, which may yield a finite 
246          result where none exists. This is not a critical bug, but an opportunity 
247          for an enhancement. 
248   
249          The definition of a raw moment in the summary is specific to the raw moment 
250          about the origin. The raw moment about any point :math:`a` is: 
251   
252          .. math:: 
253   
254              E[(X-a)^n] = \int_{\chi} (x-a)^n f(x) dx 
255   
256          In this notation, a raw moment about the origin is :math:`\mu'_n = E[x^n]`, 
257          and a central moment is :math:`\mu_n = E[(x-\mu)^n]`, where :math:`\mu` 
258          is the first raw moment; i.e. the mean. 
259   
260          The ``'transform'`` method takes advantage of the following relationships 
261          between moments taken about different points :math:`a` and :math:`b`. 
262   
263          .. math:: 
264   
265              E[(X-b)^n] =  \sum_{i=0}^n E[(X-a)^i] {n \choose i} (a - b)^{n-i} 
266   
267          For instance, to transform the raw moment to the central moment, we let 
268          :math:`b = \mu` and :math:`a = 0`. 
269   
270          The distribution infrastructure provides flexibility for distribution 
271          authors to implement separate formulas for raw moments, central moments, 
272          and standardized moments of any order. By default, the moment of the 
273          desired order and kind is evaluated from the formula if such a formula 
274          is available; if not, the infrastructure uses any formulas that are 
275          available rather than resorting directly to numerical integration. 
276          For instance, if formulas for the first three raw moments are 
277          available and the third standardized moments is desired, the 
278          infrastructure will evaluate the raw moments and perform the transforms 
279          and standardization required. The decision tree is somewhat complex, 
280          but the strategy for obtaining a moment of a given order and kind 
281          (possibly as an intermediate step due to the recursive nature of the 
282          transform formula above) roughly follows this order of priority: 
283   
284          #. Use cache (if order of same moment and kind has been calculated) 
285          #. Use formula (if available) 
286          #. Transform between raw and central moment and/or normalize to convert 
287             between central and standardized moments (if efficient) 
288          #. Use a generic result true for most distributions (if available) 
289          #. Use quadrature 
290   
291          References 
292          ---------- 
293          .. [1] Moment, *Wikipedia*, 
294                 https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_(mathematics) 
295   
296          Examples 
297          -------- 
298          Instantiate a distribution with the desired parameters: 
299   
300          >>> from scipy import stats 
301          >>> X = stats.Normal(mu=1., sigma=2.) 
302   
303          Evaluate the first raw moment: 
304   
305          >>> X.moment(order=1, kind='raw') 
306          1.0 
307          >>> X.moment(order=1, kind='raw') == X.mean() == X.mu 
308          True 
309   
310          Evaluate the second central moment: 
311   
312          >>> X.moment(order=2, kind='central') 
313          4.0 
314          >>> X.moment(order=2, kind='central') == X.variance() == X.sigma**2 
315          True 
316   
317          Evaluate the fourth standardized moment: 
318   
319          >>> X.moment(order=4, kind='standardized') 
320          3.0 
321          >>> X.moment(order=4, kind='standardized') == X.kurtosis(convention='non-excess') 
322          True 
323   
324          """  # noqa:E501
325          raise NotImplementedError()

425      @abstractmethod
426      def mode(self, *, method):
427          r"""Mode (most likely value) 
428   
429          Informally, the mode is a value that a random variable has the highest 
430          probability (density) of assuming. That is, the mode is the element of 
431          the support :math:`\chi` that maximizes the probability density 
432          function :math:`f(x)`: 
433   
434          .. math:: 
435   
436              \text{mode} = \arg\max_{x \in \chi} f(x) 
437   
438          Parameters 
439          ---------- 
440          method : {None, 'formula', 'optimization'} 
441              The strategy used to evaluate the mode. 
442              By default (``None``), the infrastructure chooses between the 
443              following options, listed in order of precedence. 
444   
445              - ``'formula'``: use a formula for the median 
446              - ``'optimization'``: numerically maximize the PDF 
447   
448              Not all `method` options are available for all distributions. 
449              If the selected `method` is not available, a ``NotImplementedError`` 
450              will be raised. 
451   
452          Returns 
453          ------- 
454          out : array 
455              The mode 
456   
457          See Also 
458          -------- 
459          mean 
460          median 
461          pdf 
462   
463          Notes 
464          ----- 
465          For some distributions 
466   
467          #. the mode is not unique (e.g. the uniform distribution); 
468          #. the PDF has one or more singularities, and it is debateable whether 
469             a singularity is considered to be in the domain and called the mode 
470             (e.g. the gamma distribution with shape parameter less than 1); and/or 
471          #. the probability density function may have one or more local maxima 
472             that are not a global maximum (e.g. mixture distributions). 
473   
474          In such cases, `mode` will 
475   
476          #. return a single value, 
477          #. consider the mode to occur at a singularity, and/or 
478          #. return a local maximum which may or may not be a global maximum. 
479   
480          If a formula for the mode is not specifically implemented for the 
481          chosen distribution, SciPy will attempt to compute the mode 
482          numerically, which may not meet the user's preferred definition of a 
483          mode. In such cases, the user is encouraged to subclass the 
484          distribution and override ``mode``. 
485   
486          References 
487          ---------- 
488          .. [1] Mode (statistics), *Wikipedia*, 
489                 https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mode_(statistics) 
490   
491          Examples 
492          -------- 
493          Instantiate a distribution with the desired parameters: 
494   
495          >>> from scipy import stats 
496          >>> X = stats.Normal(mu=1., sigma=2.) 
497   
498          Evaluate the mode: 
499   
500          >>> X.mode() 
501          1.0 
502   
503          If the mode is not uniquely defined, ``mode`` nonetheless returns a 
504          single value. 
505   
506          >>> X = stats.Uniform(a=0., b=1.) 
507          >>> X.mode() 
508          0.5 
509   
510          If this choice does not satisfy your requirements, subclass the 
511          distribution and override ``mode``: 
512   
513          >>> class BetterUniform(stats.Uniform): 
514          ...     def mode(self): 
515          ...         return self.b 
516          >>> X = BetterUniform(a=0., b=1.) 
517          >>> X.mode() 
518          1.0 
519   
520          """
521          raise NotImplementedError()

693      @abstractmethod
694      def pdf(self, x, /, *, method):
695          r"""Probability density function 
696   
697          The probability density function ("PDF"), denoted :math:`f(x)`, is the 
698          probability *per unit length* that the random variable will assume the 
699          value :math:`x`. Mathematically, it can be defined as the derivative 
700          of the cumulative distribution function :math:`F(x)`: 
701   
702          .. math:: 
703   
704              f(x) = \frac{d}{dx} F(x) 
705   
706          `pdf` accepts `x` for :math:`x`. 
707   
708          Parameters 
709          ---------- 
710          x : array_like 
711              The argument of the PDF. 
712          method : {None, 'formula', 'logexp'} 
713              The strategy used to evaluate the PDF. By default (``None``), the 
714              infrastructure chooses between the following options, listed in 
715              order of precedence. 
716   
717              - ``'formula'``: use a formula for the PDF itself 
718              - ``'logexp'``: evaluate the log-PDF and exponentiate 
719   
720              Not all `method` options are available for all distributions. 
721              If the selected `method` is not available, a ``NotImplementedError`` 
722              will be raised. 
723   
724          Returns 
725          ------- 
726          out : array 
727              The PDF evaluated at the argument `x`. 
728   
729          See Also 
730          -------- 
731          cdf 
732          logpdf 
733   
734          Notes 
735          ----- 
736          Suppose a continuous probability distribution has support :math:`[l, r]`. 
737          By definition of the support, the PDF evaluates to its minimum value 
738          of :math:`0` outside the support; i.e. for :math:`x < l` or 
739          :math:`x > r`. The maximum of the PDF may be less than or greater than 
740          :math:`1`; since the valus is a probability *density*, only its integral 
741          over the support must equal :math:`1`. 
742   
743          References 
744          ---------- 
745          .. [1] Probability density function, *Wikipedia*, 
746                 https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_density_function 
747   
748          Examples 
749          --------

1410         r"""Log of the complementary cumulative distribution function 
1411  
1412         The complementary cumulative distribution function ("CCDF"), denoted 
1413         :math:`G(x)` is the complement of the cumulative distribution function 
1414         :math:`F(x)`; i.e., probability the random variable :math:`X` will 
1415         assume a value greater than :math:`x`: 
1416  
1417         .. math:: 
1418  
1419             G(x) = 1 - F(x) = P(X > x) 
1420  
1421         A two-argument variant of this function is: 
1422  
1423         .. math:: 
1424  
1425             G(x, y) = 1 - F(x, y) = P(X < x \quad \text{or} \quad X > y) 
1426  
1427         `logccdf` computes the logarithm of the complementary cumulative 
1428         distribution function ("log-CCDF"), :math:`\log(G(x))`/:math:`\log(G(x, y))`, 
1429         but it may be numerically favorable compared to the naive implementation 
1430         (computing the CDF and taking the logarithm). 
1431  
1432         `logccdf` accepts `x` for :math:`x` and `y` for :math:`y`. 
1433  
1434         Parameters 
1435         ---------- 
1436         x, y : array_like 
1437             The arguments of the log-CCDF. `x` is required; `y` is optional. 
1438         method : {None, 'formula', 'logexp', 'complement', 'quadrature', 'addition'} 
1439             The strategy used to evaluate the log-CCDF. 
1440             By default (``None``), the one-argument form of the function 
1441             chooses between the following options, listed in order of precedence. 
1442  
1443             - ``'formula'``: use a formula for the log CCDF itself 
1444             - ``'logexp'``: evaluate the CCDF and take the logarithm 
1445             - ``'complement'``: evaluate the log-CDF and take the 
1446               logarithmic complement (see Notes) 
1447             - ``'quadrature'``: numerically log-integrate the log-PDF 
1448  
1449             The two-argument form chooses between: 
1450  
1451             - ``'formula'``: use a formula for the log CCDF itself 
1452             - ``'addition'``: compute the log-CDF at `x` and the log-CCDF at `y`, 
1453               then take the logarithmic sum (see Notes)

1457             will be raised.

1467         logcdf

1472         The log-CCDF returns its minimum value of :math:`\log(0)=-\infty` for

1484         and related limitations of floating point numbers.
